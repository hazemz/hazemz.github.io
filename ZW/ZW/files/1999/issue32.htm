Radio Jordan Interviews Alia and Reema

Celebrating the Kids Broadcasting Day, Radio Jordan's Safana Bustami interviewed Alia and Reema by phone on Friday, December 10, 1999.  Following are the two interviews:

Alia's Interview

Q. Alia, tell us about your disability.

A. I suffer from Cerebral Palsy, which is when the nerve cells in the brain are damaged.  Therefore, as a consequence, I have motor difficulties.  I walk on two sticks, and I also wear hearing aids.

***

Q. Alia, you went to regular school and now you’re studying at university.  Tell me, how did you manage to cope with all that?

A. At first, it was very difficult because people reacted to me in different ways.  I was regarded, at first, as a disabled person and not as an individual.  But, as time went by, they began to understand that I’m a human being with the same emotional needs of everybody else, and we actually started to change; they started to accept me, I began having equal opportunities, equal chances, I never felt any different.

***

Q.  Who do you feel gave the most support while you were growing up?

A. I believe my parents’ continuous love, support, and encouragement helped me a great deal.  They accepted the situation, and they taught me how to accept the situation, and they were persistent.  They told me that everything has a reward through hard work.  They told me the truth; they never lied to me.  So, yes, I think in that way I’ve come a long way.

***

Q. Alia, can you please tell me about the most memorable moment in your life.

A. That would have to be when I was about ten.  It was when I took my first step when I was doing physiotherapy.  It was the first moment I took my first step.  It was very, very memorable, very, very special.

***

Q. How understanding do you feel people are to your disability?

A. As I said before, back then, I had a lot of negative responses.  People only saw my disability and never saw me as a person, and this is changing because people are learning that disabled people are human beings, firstly and foremost.  Now, I think society in Jordan is in the process of changing, but we still have a long way to go.

***

Q. So you are the Representative of Disabled Persons on campus.  How do you come about this title?

A. Basically, in the first year, the Student Union at Royal Holloway elected the Disabled Representative, and this representative speaks on behalf of all other disabled people.  We are here to make sure that equal rights and equal opportunities are given to each and every student to make sure that disabled people have their needs met.  And unfortunately, because the campus in not 100% accessible, we have to work around accessibility in this area.

***

Q. Alia, what are your future plans?

A. I’d like to pursue a Masters degree in International Law and hopefully continue to be an advocate for the rights of disabled people in Jordan.  

---------------------------

Reema's Interview

Q. We just heard Alia talking.  Tell us, how do you feel about this?

A. At this moment, I’m very happy.  I think we’re getting a lot of results of hard work, persistence, and achievement of Alia.  Alia now is a functional person.  She’s productive.  She’s integrated.  She has the right of choices, and she has a life of her own and hopes to the future.

*** 

Q. Alia’s mother is a member of the Family and Friends Society of Persons with Disabilities.  Tell us about this society and the need to have it.

A. Our society is a support group.  A lot of parents suffer from denial, shocks, depression of having a child with disabilities.  Now we are concerned with the awareness issues.  Early acceptance, early detection, and intervention lead to better results.  Quality rehabilitation might avoid forming secondary disabilities.  A lot of parents get coiled up with their depression and fear of the unknown.  They don’t know how to handle such a situation, thus wasting the first golden years of a child’s life, which are the first five very important years for the child.  So we are a support group.  We have parents who feel ashamed of having a child with disabilities.  They are stigmatized.  There are negative attitudes towards the child with disabilities.  We form a support group to the parents in this respect.  We try to tell them that they are human beings, in the first place, with a disability.  They have to enjoy their rights.  A lot of them are ignorant of their rights; they’re ignorant of the Law for the Welfare of Disabled Persons for the year 1993.  On the other hand, in this respect, we are a pressure group to enforce the law and to enforce the rights.  And a lot of parents, unfortunately, are quite ignorant of all this.

***

Q. How did you become involved in this group?

A. I’m a mother, in the first place, and you’re talking about 21 years ago, at the time when there were no support groups what so ever, there was no proper rehabilitation at the time, and I had to be trained how to work with my daughter, physiotherapy in particular, and I had to perform it daily.  It was hard work.  It was quite challenging, but, thank God, we managed to reach a stage whereby Alia is functional now.  And I wanted to work in support of those who are unprivileged.  And they don’t have the support of their family that I had.  I had all the support of the family.  I had all the understanding of my family, Alia as well, and this helped a lot.  I believe we can be good advocates to such issues as acceptance and getting rid of this stigma.

***

Q. What sort of activities has the society been involved with?

A. To start with, we have a parents meeting.  It works like a group therapy.  They exchange their ideas.  They exchange their experiences.  We have programs like the Tuesday School, which caters for the physically handicapped, and the Friendship Club, which caters for the mentally handicapped.  They do a lot of activities like music, gardening, arts, cooking, daily living activities.  A lot of the children would love to boil a cup of coffee, but they are forbidden simply because the mother in living in fear.  They are overprotective for their children.  We have a preschool club for the four year olds till the six year olds, whereby they come, and they have individual programs to meet each and every child’s needs.  We have services like therapeutic horse riding; we are members in the Jordanian Therapeutic Horse Riding Club.  And last, but not least, we have the Portage, which is training mothers at home, whereby a trained home visitor visits the mother of a child.  The targeted group is mentally challenged children from the date of birth till nine years of age.  The home visitor trains the mother on how to handle her child according to an educational kit.  It’s an early intervention program, and our unit is quite successful.

***

Q. Reema Zureikat, what advice would you give youth and children who have some form of disability?

A. To deal with the matter as a fact.  The sooner you accept the disability, the better you are.  And to realize that you are a human being and there is an ability rather than to look at the disability.  Early acceptance and to believe in yourself.

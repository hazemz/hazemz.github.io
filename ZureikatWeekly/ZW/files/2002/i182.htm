"Olive Picking Season" Continues at the Shomalis

Bana: The Shomalis spent three days picking the olives in the orchard

 

In her weekly e-mail to the Editor in Chief, Bana updated us on the latest regarding the olive picking season at the Shomalis.  In her e-mail, Bana said, "Olive picking season, once again, at the Shomali House.  The Shomalis spent three days picking the olives in the orchard. The hardest workers, of course, were Abla and Basil, and Basil's aching back is a proof of it.  It took three consecutive days to pick the olives, sort them into bags, and now they are awaiting at the Shomali doorstep for their turn at the "ma3sara" to be crushed.  As this year's olive harvest has been very abundant, it is very difficult to find a place at the "ma3sara".  It looks like Basil will be spending a few nights at the factory waiting for his turn."

"ZW Photographers, or is it?"

~ Marwan Zureikat

Dear Editor in Chief,

Thanks for publishing my last letter to you.  Reading the last issue, I am now wondering whether we have been spending a lot of time and effort on ZW Photographers ignoring the more important element for the success of the ZW.  I am referring, of course, to the Weekly Correspondents.  I was particularly alarmed by some of the news that appeared in the last issue.  Some were irrelevant, others were not news at all, but, and this is the most important, none of them went beyond scratching the surface.  I am always  fascinated by how CNN keeps emphasizing that its correspondents do not merely report the event, but try hard to dig into it to bring about the "hidden details and analysis".

If, for some, this appears like "talking theories", I refer to the lunch at the Karadshehs on November 8.  In the ZW, the news of this event was a mere listing of people and dishes.  None of the correspondents present during lunch bothered to ask why the vegetables looked so clean and shining, or why everything looked like we are being served Mansaf when the taste of Mansaf was not there.  For those who do not know, the Karadshehs go through a very tedious and long process of cleaning the vegetables before serving them.  Most vegetables are washed several times, then pealed, then washed after pealing, after which they are kept in a container filled with water and a dry-cleaning agent for several hours before being served.  I wouldn't have gathered all this information had it not been for the fact that I have noticed, unlike all the ZW Correspondents present, the  ultra-shining look of the vegetables served, particularly the carrots and the cucumbers.  The Mansaf issue took some more effort from me to find out.  It wasn't until the next day when I had a chance to sit with Abla Z., who happened to sit with the Karadshehs that same morning.  She told me that she couldn't help noticing Naief adding water to the Mansaf on several occasions.  The poor Jameed must have given in at last.

This letter  to you, Mr. Editor, is not intended to instigate you to make any decisions to fire or hire Correspondents, but, merely, as always, to share things with the rest of the Zureikats.

Best regards,
Marwan Zureikat

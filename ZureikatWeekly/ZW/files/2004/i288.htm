Ziad Presides Over Conference on Cancer Prevention and Early Detection


Ziad and Their Royal Highnesses Prince Talal and Princess Gheida at the opening session. Amal can also be seen in the second row.
(Courtesy of the Jordan News Agency (Petra))

Ziad presided over the first conference on "Cancer Prevention and Early Detection", which was launched in Amman on Thursday, November 25, 2004.  The opening session of the conference was held at the Grand Hyatt Hotel in Amman and was patronized by Their Royal Highnesses Prince Talal bin Mohammad and Princess Gheida Talal.  Reema and Amal attended a number of sessions in the conference.  Following are some details from the Friday-Saturday, November 26-27, 2004, edition of The Jordan Times:

“Cancer has come to be seen as a death sentence, and people avoid submitting to cancer tests for fear of discovering that they have it... The public should know that cancer is not a death sentence; that it can be cured,” said Senator Ziad Sharaiha, president of the conference, who is also the president of the Private physicians Association.

The aim of this gathering is to raise public awareness, through lectures and interactive sessions, where the public can ask questions about the disease, Sharaiha told reporters.

“It is important that doctors are aware of the importance of early detection and prevention, and how to spot tumors, because it is their responsibility to inform their patients, that is why such a conference is important for both doctors and patients,” Sharaiha explained.

Local News:

  • Prince Hamzah has been relieved of his responsibilities as Crown Prince. "I have decided to free you from the constraints of the position of Crown Prince in order to give you the freedom to work and undertake any mission or responsibility I entrust you with, alongside with all our brothers, the sons of Al Hussein, and other members of the Hashemite Family,' King Abdullah told the Prince in a letter he sent on Sunday. The King continued: "As for the position of Crown Prince, I will continue, guided by the Constitution and the good of our beloved Jordan and our noble Hashemite message, to give it my sincere attention."

  • The European Union on Thursday granted Jordan 30 million euros to help the Kingdom alleviate poverty and develop underprivileged areas. The agreement of the grant, which is to aid local community projects, was signed during a meeting between His Majesty King Abdullah and European Commission (EC) President Jose Manuel Barroso in Brussels.

  • President of the Palestine Liberation Organisation Mahmoud Abbas, Palestinian Authority (PA) interim chairman, Rawhi Fattouh, and Prime Minister Ahmed Qureia arrived in Amman on Sunday.

  • Authorities continued combating isolated waves of locusts in the Ghor Al Safi area and Tafileh Governorate, saying they do not expect to see any new waves entering the country any time soon. The waves of locusts spotted in Ghor Al Safi and the southern Governorate of Tafileh have been totally eradicated, according to Minister of Agriculture Sharari Shakhanbeh.

  • The Kingdom is gearing up for the development of a new private, non-profit American university on Aqaba's southern shores, with a sister campus in Amman. The Aqaba Development Corporation (ADC) and the Aqaba Special Economic Zone (ASEZ) signed an agreement this week for the university's construction on 250 dunums of land on the Red Sea coastal shore.

World News:

  • The Fatah movement nominated Mahmoud Abbas for the January 9 Palestinian presidential elections. Meanwhile, Marwan Barghouti, who is currently jailed in Israel, announced he will not be running and publicly called for supporting Abbas.

  • Israel is prepared to coordinate its pullout from Gaza with a new Palestinian government, officials said Sunday, a shift from Prime Minister Ariel Sharon's concept of "unilateral disengagement" and a sign that cooperation may be restored in the post-Arafat era.

  • Iraq's landmark January polls looked set to take place as planned, although the death of five US troops in one province and the violence in the rest of the country did nothing to alleviate the security concerns of parties seeking a delay.

  • Palestinian security officials said Saturday they were dismantling a squad, known as "the death group," in what appeared to be the first concrete move towards reforming the sprawling security forces.

  • The son of UN Secretary General Kofi Annan got monthly payments more than four years longer than was previously known from a Swiss firm that won a lucrative contract under the scandal-ridden UN oil-for-food program, the United Nations said on Friday.

  • At least 25 miners were killed and some 140 were trapped Sunday when a gas explosion ripped through a coal mine in northern China, in what could be the worst disaster to hit the world's biggest coal producer in recent years.

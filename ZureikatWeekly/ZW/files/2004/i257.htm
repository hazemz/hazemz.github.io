Bana Shomali - Amman, Jordan - April 21, 2004:

    The preparations for the jaha started a week before the big day.  300 cheese rolls were rolled, 300 meat puffs were puffed, a wide variety of tossed salads were tossed, and an endless amount of warag dawali was twirled and tucked all by the relentless hands of Abla, Teta Umm Basil, Majdi, Astuti and Ayouni.  The Arabic coffee stood brewing at the Shomali stove for five days, an intricate operation led by Majdi, whose special coffee is renowned among the families of Beit Sahour.  The rest of us handled the logistics of that evening, through running an inventory of the plates, forks, knives and chairs, to make sure that both food and comfort were in abundance that night.
    On the evening of April 20, the jaha started pouring into our house at 7:00pm, where Basil, Abla and Bashar greeted the men with Majdi’s freshly brewed coffee.  At 7:30pm, the fleet of cars led by Basil and Bashar left toward Rana’s grandfather’s house, Abu Mash-hour el-Dababneh.  A small glitch occurred when we realized that we had forgotten Yacoub at home and had to send off one of Bashar’s friends back to retrieve him.  Yacoub’s pride was hurt, but soon recovered toward the end of the night.  At the Dababneh house, yet another surprise was waiting for us.  Harran, caught by surprise, was asked to speak for the Shomalis by Basil’s uncle.  Harran skillfully mastered the shock, and improvised a warm and heart-filled speech, and asked for Rana’s hand from the Dababnehs.  The request was received with a quick approval, and was followed by trays and trays of straight whiskey shots for the jaha (a unique, but pleasantly intoxicating, Beit Sahouri tradition).  Bashar and Rana later exchanged their wedding rings in a simple ceremony as a sign of commitment.
    The Shomalis and Zureikats later returned to our house for drinks and appetizers.  Umm Basil initiated the celebration with a series of “haa haat” and “awweehat”, and traditional Sahouri singing, dancing and handkerchief waving.  Reema also contributed with her zaghareet and her version of “haa haat” which she had conveniently scribbled on a small piece of paper just in case she forgot the lines.  The duo of Umm Basil and Reema was soon interrupted when the loud echo of men reciting “Tlawlahi ya Dalieh” came flooding into the house.  Bashar’s friends had greeted Bashar and Rana at the steps of the house, and threw a spontaneous mini-zaffeh for the couple on their way into the house.  The rest of the invitees joined the zaffeh, and the echoes of singing and the tableh could be heard across the neighborhood.  Abu Farid Shomali, Bashar’s godfather, also recited the original version of “Natt il Biss 3ala Dahir il Cheess”, a traditional Sahouri song written by Abu Farid’s grandfather.
    The night continued with lots of drinking, eating, and spontaneous bursts of “haa haat” here and there.  All in all, it was a successful night, due to the efforts of everyone in the family, Shomalis and Zureikats, be it in the preparations, or in the moral and emotional support extended to the tense couple, Bashar and Rana, not to mention Bashar’s parents.  One should also mention that the night was missing a few, but very important people, namely Jiddo, who had excused himself from attending the party, the Karadshehs, the Hennings, Hassan, Amer and Leanne, Dana and Ammar, Hazem, the Sharaihas (Ziad, Reem, Samer, Ayman and Talal), and last, but not least, Kareem Zureikat.
    The Shomalis would like to thank everyone for coming to the party and would like to extend their congratulations to Bashar and Rana.  We welcome Rana to the family and to the Zureikat Weekly.  We wish the new couple a future filled with success, happiness, and interesting editions of the ZW.

    And The Zureikat Weekly would also like to take this opportunity to congratulate Bashar and Rana on their jaha, wishing them all the happiness and success.

Letter from Marwan to the Editor in Chief

Marwan sent the following letter to the Editor in Chief:

Dear Editor,

Chandra has been away now for two weeks.  It is quiet at home without Chandra, more so than in her past vacations, given that Tarek is "independent" and Hazem is away.  Abla is attending to the housekeeping with little help from me.  She does the laundry and very little (near to nothing) cooking.  The folks at Houston's are not complaining anymore that they do not see me as often as they like.

Speaking of doing the laundry, it was late in the evening two weeks ago when we were both watching television.  Abla suddenly sprung from her chair, approached me, and bent under my chair as if she was examining something.  She then went back to where she was sitting, brought her glasses, and repeated the same act with me wondering what the hell she was doing.  It was a few seconds before she declared that "she thinks" that I am wearing socks of two different colors, one black and one dark brown.  I glanced at my socks and was quick to disagree with her.  We argued for a few minutes before I decided to take my socks off for further examination.  Lights in the living room were not bright enough to reach a conclusive decision, and we had to go to the kitchen (with the socks, of course) where it was bright enough to enable both of us to agree conclusively: YES, they were of different colors.

It would seem to you, Mr. Editor, and the rest of the ZW members that this is one typical and common story that happens often with many people.  Actually, this was my first reaction until I concluded that since the socks that I am wearing are of two different colors, then it is only logical that there is another pair in my closet that Abla had folded that is composed of two different colors.  For the past two weeks, I have been living the agony of having to check each pair every day.  Do it myself first with the lights in the bedroom, check with Abla for further confirmation, and often having to go to the kitchen window for a conclusive decision.  It was only until this morning that we celebrated the apprehension of that pair.

Marwan Zureikat

'No News is Good News'...or Is It?

A message from the Editor in Chief

"The family's failure to contribute...will result in the discontinuation of the newsletter in the very near future."

Many of you might have noticed that the last few issues of The Zureikat Weekly were very short.  In general, I would not expect every single week to be eventful.  After all, we are only a family; how much news can we make in one week?  But the problem is not the lack of news, it is, rather, the lack of cooperation from the ZW Correspondents.  Many correspondents have not sent any e-mails in more than a month.  Others who did not have any news sent e-mails saying 'no news to report for this week', which I very much appreciate.

I would like to take this opportunity to urge all correspondents to send weekly e-mails to the ZW by the specified deadline.  Even if there were no news for a certain week, a correspondent should send an e-mail.  I would also like to ask other members of the family to send in their news, comments and suggestions.  Since appointing the Correspondents three years ago, e-mails from other family members haven't been coming in as they used to.

Again, this is not about the lack of news.  It is about the lack of correspondence.  The ZW depends on your input now more than ever.  The family's failure to contribute to the Weekly will result in the discontinuation of the newsletter in the very near future.

Thank you.

Editor in Chief,
Hazem Zureiqat

Local

  • King Abdullah II left to the US on Monday.  The King also returned from a visit to Omar last week.

  • Public and private school students in the Kingdom will have their school days increased by 11 days, from 182 to 193, starting the next academic year.  Under the Ministry of Education decision, students will begin their 2005/2006 scholastic year on Aug. 24 instead of Sept. 1 and their final exams will be rescheduled four days later than usual.

  • A reporter for Al Ghad daily was interrogated on Monday by authorities for writing "false and fabricated news" claiming that a citizen carried out a deadly suicide attack south of Baghdad last month, an official said.

International

  • Nearly one million opposition protesters gathered in Beirut on Monday a month after former PM Rafik Hariri's killing.  The protest came six days after hundreds of thousands of Lebanese flooded central Beirut on Tuesday for a pro-Syrian rally called by Hizbollah.  On the other hand, a senior Lebanese army officer said Sunday that 4,000 Syrian soldiers — more than a quarter of those serving in Lebanon just a week ago — are back in Syria, but he said a complete withdrawal is nearly a month away at the earliest.   Meanwhile, Omar Karami, who was forced to resign a couple of weeks ago by a wave of popular opposition, returned to his post Thursday riding the counter wave of a thronging pro-Syrian demonstration this week.

  • Kurdish leaders decided Sunday to go ahead and tie up loose ends in their deal with the Shiites to help form a coalition government when Iraq's first democratically elected parliament in modern history convenes this week.

  • UN chief Kofi Annan, on his first visit to the Middle East in four years, on Sunday hailed moves aimed at reviving the peace process as Israel reportedly decided to tear down rogue settler outposts.

  • Egypt ordered the release on bail on Saturday of Ayman Nour, an opposition leader detained since the end of January, the public prosecutor said.

ZW to be Discontinued in 4 Weeks

6th anniversary issue, to be released on April 24, will be the last

Decision comes after a longstanding debate on the future of the family newsletter

********************************************

Announcement by the Editor in Chief

Dear Zureikats,

After careful thought and consideration of current circumstances, I have decided to discontinue The Zureikat Weekly after four weeks.  The 309th issue, which will be released on April 24, 2005, one day after the newsletter's 6th anniversary, will be the final issue.

Although many of you might disagree with this decision, I believe it is for the best.  The newsletter has come a long way in the past six years, but the family has also grown and changed.  Each of us now has his or her own priorities, and smaller Zureikat families now have their own "kingdoms", to use Marwan's words.

Finally, it has been a pleasure producing this newsletter for the past six years.  I thank all those who supported the ZW and wish you all, all the best.

Editor in Chief,
Hazem Zureikat

A Longstanding Debate

The debate over the future of The Zureikat Weekly has been present for a long time.  It probably started before the Editor in Chief left to the US in 2002, but the height of it came in November 2003 when Marwan sent an open letter suggesting the discontinuation of the family newsletter.  The suggestion drew harsh criticism from most of the family, after which the debate ended.

Following is the full text of Marwan's letter to the Editor in Chief, which was published in the 233rd issue (released on November 9, 2003):

Dear Hazem,

I have hesitated a lot before I decided to write this letter to you.  I have been regularly reading The Zureikat Weekly issues and never stopped admiring the effort and the dedication that is needed to keep this web magazine running weekly, non-stop, for over 4.5 years.  However, I consider it the right time now to consider stopping the issue of the Weekly and/or consider other options to maintain flow of the family news and information.

It is no secret that the Weekly was intended to maintain and keep our family culture and values.  It is not a coincidence that the Weekly was thought of and initiated by you after the passing away of your grandmother.  It was one expression of your and our desire to keep her memory and what she stood for alive and living with us.  But with the passing of time, things change.  The family grows, and people age.  The bonds that used to tie the family become overshadowed by other priorities and interests.  It is a fact now that some of us have no relationship with the Weekly at all.  Others do not read it, and many do not contribute to it.  To keep forcing the Weekly would, in my mind, be like working against the tide and may result in negative consequences.  It is great to maintain and keep our family values and culture, but this is something that we should not force and must leave for each of the smaller Zureikat family units to deal with in the manner they deem convenient to them.

The Zureikat Weekly has done a great job.  Its significance and value are huge.  In the future, we will all look back at it and be proud of it.  It will serve as an example for those who will follow us.  For me, it has shown, more than anything else, how much of a difference a single individual can make given the dedication and commitment that you have shown.  I am certainly a proud father and a proud Zureikat.

I leave it to you to consider this issue and make whatever decision you deem appropriate.

Marwan Zureikat

Local

  • More than 1,000 Orthodox Christians held a vigil on Friday to protest the reported sale of church land in the Holy City of Jerusalem to Jewish organizations.  The peaceful demonstration followed a recent report in the Israeli daily Maariv that the Greek Orthodox Church sold church land, including two hotels, in the traditionally Palestinian section in the Old City of Jerusalem to Jewish groups working to bolster the Jewish presence in the area.

  • Jordan won the Franklin Delano Roosevelt International Disability Award.  King Abdullah II received the award on behalf of Jordan in a ceremony held at the UN headquarters in New York.  The King returned to Amman the next day.  During the last week of his US visit, the King met with Secretary of State Condoleezza Rice and received an honorary doctorate in literature and humanities from Georgetown University.

  • King Abdullah ordered the immediate return of Amman's top diplomat to Iraq to ease tensions between the two countries.

International

  • Two blasts rocked Beirut in the past week.  The first happened on Wednesday when a powerful bomb tore through a shopping mall near Jounieh, killing three Asian workers.  The second was on Saturday when a bomb exploded in an industrial zone of a mainly Christian neighborhood northeast of Beirut, wounding three people.  Meanwhile, the Syrian president was reported Thursday to be close to announcing a timetable for the final withdrawal of his forces from Lebanon, a move seen as critical to the organization of Lebanese parliamentary elections in May.

  • Arab leaders steered clear of the region's most contentious issues, wrapping up a summit in Algiers Wednesday with a rehashed Middle East peace plan that Israel and Palestinian militant groups swiftly rejected.  Algerian President Abdulaziz Bouteflika said in a closing statement that the summit, marred by the absence of several key players, "consolidated the bases of a durable Arab reconciliation."

  • One of Egypt's leading actors, Ahmed Zaki, has died at the age of 55 in Cairo after a long illness.  The film star had been suffering from lung cancer since early last year and had been in a coma for several weeks.
